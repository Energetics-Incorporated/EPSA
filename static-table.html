Description:

Class I hazardous wells in Wyoming are administered by the Wyoming Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) under provisions of the federal Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA). Wyoming DEQ’s Underground Injection Control Program (UIC) must meet the minimum requirements established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (see Class I Hazardous Underground Injection Control Program [Federal]) to establish and maintain primacy over the state program as per Title 40 CFR, § 144.1(b). The construction, conversion, use, and operation of an injection well is prohibited unless authorized by a permit authorized by the Wyoming DEQ. No permit shall be issued where an injection well causes or allows the movement of fluid that would result in the pollution of an underground source of drinking water (USDW). In addition a separate sage grouse compliance form must accompany any permit to drill a new UIC class I well. The US EPA granted the State of Wyoming a formal aquifer exemption for the Minnelusa Formation located in the central part of the Powder River Basin. Class I wells injecting into this portion of the Minnelusa Formation do not require a separate aquifer exemption. Wyoming Regulations for UIC Class 1 wells closely mirrors the federal UIC regulation as written in Title 40 CFR, § 144-148. Class I hazardous wells must comply with all Wyoming DNR requirements for Class I non-hazardous wells (see Class I Non-Hazardous Underground Injection Control Program [Wyoming]). Area of Review: • The Area of review for Class 1 hazardous waste wells is the greater of 2 miles, the cone of influence from the injection well or the total area of emplaced waste. Siting and Construction Requirements: • Well must be cemented all the way from the receiving formation to the surface Operations and Monitoring: • The operator shall maintain a casing/tubing annulus pressure that exceeds the operating injection pressure, unless the administrator determines that such a requirement might harm the integrity of the well. The fluid used in the casing/tubing annulus shall be noncorrosive, and shall contain a corrosion inhibitor. • The operator shall install, maintain, and use continuous recording devices to monitor the injection pressure, flow rate, temperature, of injected fluids and pressure on the casing/tubing annulus, and shall install and use automatic alarm and shut-off systems designed to shut down the well when pressures, flow rates, and other parameters approved by the administrator exceed the range specified in the permit. • The operator must have a trained operator onsite at all times the well is operating.

Description:

In Wyoming, the UIC program for Class II wells is administered by the Wyoming Oil and Gas Conservation Commission under Section 1425 of the federal Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA). As per Section 1425 of the SDWA, the Wyoming Oil and Gas Conservation Commission’s UIC program must demonstrate that its standards are effective in preventing endangerment of USDWs to establish and maintain primacy over the state program. All new Class II well must be permitted, including construction, conversion and operation of a well. No well operation should endanger fresh water sources, and all disposal wells must be cased and cemented such that damage is not caused to oil, gas, or fresh water sources. Operating Requirements: The mechanical integrity of the well must be established no less than once every five years. a mechanical integrity test of an injection well is a test designed to determine: if there is a significant leak in the casing, tubing, or packer of the well, and if there is significant fluid movement into an underground source of drinking water through vertical channels adjacent to the wellbore. The following methods can be used to establish the mechanical integrity of a well: pressure test; cementing records; or other approved tests. A well that lacks mechanical integrity test must be shut-in immediately. Variances can be requested if the operator is able to demonstrate that the leaks do not pose a threat to any USDWs. Variances may be granted to the regulations for standard fresh water protection for good cause and to provide that unnecessary hardship not be imposed. All such requests must demonstrate that the variance will in no way endanger fresh water. Aquifer Exemption: An aquifer may be designated as exempt is it meets the follow criteria: • It is mineral, hydrocarbon, or geothermal energy producing; • It is situated at a depth or location which makes recovery of fresh and potable water economically or technologically impractical; • It is so contaminated that it would be economically or technologically impractical to render the water fit for use as fresh and potable water; • It is located over a mining area subject to subsidence or catastrophic collapse; or, • It has a total dissolved solids of more than 5000 and less than 10,000 mg/l and is not reasonably expected to be used as fresh or potable water. No exempt aquifers listed as the information that is publicly available on exempt aquifers is insufficient, but this does not necessarily indicate that there are no exempt aquifers in the state.

Description:

Wyoming recognizes the "prior appropriation" doctrine for groundwater, and the Wyoming State Engineer's Office issues permits for surface water rights. Applications are evaluated based on whether the proposed use will interfere with existing water rights, the water will be used for a beneficial use, and the water right will harm the public welfare. The permittee has three years to put the water to use after the permit is approved. If the water right holder does not use the water for beneficial use for five successive years, the water right is considered abandoned. The owner of a water right may change the location of a well, change the use, or change the place of use with permission of the Board of Control or State Engineer. In a designated groundwater control area, the State Engineer may restrict groundwater withdrawal. Depending on the water supply issues in the area, the State Engineer may use one of the following corrective controls: restrict further appropriation of groundwater; determine a permissible total withdrawal and apportion this total withdrawal among water rights holders; order junior rights holders to cease or reduce withdrawals; and/or enforce a system of rotation of use of groundwater. The State Engineer may authorize temporary acquisition of adjudicated or valid unadjudicated water rights for exploratory drilling operations or other temporary purposes. A Temporary Water Use Agreement is a contract between an appropriator of an existing water right and user to allow a temporary change of use of a water right to occur for a maximum of two years.

Description:

The Boysen Dam and Reservoir Regulation manual (hereafter referred to as Water Control Manual, or WCM) presents a detailed plan for water control and management at the Boysen Dam and Reservoir (the Project), located in Fremont County, Wyoming, on the Bighorn River, 295 miles upstream of the confluence of the Bighorn River with the Yellowstone River near Bighorn, Montana. Authorized project purposes are: - Flood control - Hydropower - Irrigation - Silt control The primary function of the Project is to regulate the flow of the Bighorn River, so that irrigation and power storage will not only provide a firm irrigation supply for new downstream irrigation, but will protect existing downstream water rights so that additional upstream diversions may be made. The Boysen Dam and Reservoir WCM was developed and is maintained by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE), but the Project itself is owned and operated by the US Department of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation (USBR). USACE is responsible for use of reservoir storage allocated for flood control and navigation, but the Project is otherwise operated by USBR for power, irrigation, sanitation, recreation, and other conservation purposes. The total reservoir storage capacity of the Project is 1.49 million acre-feet (485.5 billion gallons). Regulations relevant to electricity generation are summarized below. Hydropower: The Project’s hydroelectric power plant consists of two units with a combined installed capacity of 15 MW. Approximately 260,000 acre-feet (84.7 billion gallons) of storage are required for minimum power pool. Releases for power generation are expected to average approximately 1,200 cubic feet per second (cfs), which will also satisfy the irrigation and other downstream conservation requirements. Releases made through the power plant for hydroelectric generation can accommodate up to 2,400 cfs. Flood Control: Total flood storage capacity at the Project is 300,000 acre-feet (97.8 billion gallons), 150,000 acre-feet (48.9 billion gallons) of which is exclusive flood control storage and 150,000 acre-feet (48.9 billion gallons) of which is joint-use storage. The primary purpose of the joint-use space is for multi-purpose conservation uses, and consequently regulation must be such that a reasonably assured fill is possible before releases in excess of conservation requirements are made. However, between February 1 and July 31 of each year a portion of the storage in the Project’s joint-use pool shall be kept available for flood control purposes. USACE is responsible for the Project’s use of reservoir storage allocated for flood control, as set forth in a statement of understanding between USACE and USBR regarding the regulation of flood control storage in Boysen Reservoir and operation of Boysen Dam for flood control. Whenever necessary in the interest of flood control, a USACE representative will issue instructions to USBR for coordination of the flood control operation of the Project (either for storage or evacuation purposes) with flood conditions and the operation of other reservoirs or other flood protection projects within the Bighorn, Yellowstone, or Missouri River basins. Drought Contingency Plan: There is no separate Drought Contingency Plan for the Project.

The WYPDES program for stormwater requires facilities to develop stormwater pollution prevention plans (SWPPPs) with site-specific prevention and treatment best management practices (BMPs). SWPPPs are written assessments of potential sources of pollutants in stormwater runoff and control measures that each facility will implement to minimize the discharge of these pollutants in runoff from the site. BMPs in SWPPPs must be selected and implemented to address good housekeeping practices, minimizing exposure, erosion and sediment control, and management of runoff. Oil and gas production activities and processing and treatment operations and transmission facilities that discharge stormwater that has been contaminated by contact with raw material, intermediate or finished products, by-products, or waste products are required to obtain WYPDES permits for stormwater discharge. This requirement applies to facilities/activities that have discharged stormwater that has contributed to a violation of a water quality standard or has discharged a reportable quantity of: 1) oil, including grease: reportable quantity defined as discharges that violate applicable water quality standards or cause a film or sheen upon or discoloration of the surface of water or adjoining shorelines, or result in deposition of a sludge or emulsion beneath the surface of the water or upon adjoining shorelines; or 2) hazardous substances, defined in 40 CFR 117.3 and 40 CFR 302.4. Common activities and associated pollutants that may be covered under SWPPPs at oil and gas extraction facilities include: • Construction (e.g. total suspended solids (TSS), total dissolved solids (TDS), and oil and grease) • Well drilling (e.g. TSS, TDS, oil and grease, chemical oxygen demand (COD), chlorides, barium, naphthalene, benzene, lead, arsenic, and fluoride) • Well completion/stimulation (e.g. TSS, TDS, oil and grease, COD, acid, acetone, toluene, ethanol, and xylenes) • Production (e.g. chlorides, TDS, oil and grease, TSS, pH, benzene, [henanthrene, barium, arsenic, lead, and antimony) • Vehicle and equipment cleaning and repairing (e.g. TSS, TDS, oil and grease, and pH) • Site closures (e.g. TSS, TDS, oil and grease, and pH) • Vehicle fueling (e.g. TSS, TDS, and oil and grease) Discharge of stormwater must also adhere to the federal effluent limitation guidelines and state thermal effluent limitations, which are outlined in the following database entries: National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System Permitting Program - Oil and Gas Production Wastewater (Federal), Wyoming Pollutant Discharge Elimination System Permitting Program - Oil and Gas Production Wastewater (Wyoming), and Water Quality Standards - Temperature (Wyoming).

The Wyoming Department of Environmental Quality uses the federally established effluent limitation guidelines (ELGs) for developing TBELs for wastewater discharge. However, in instances where TBELs are not adequate to meet the water quality standards of the body of water into which the facility is discharging, WQBELs are developed for each pollutant in the power plant's effluent. Water quality standards are established by Wyoming and approved by the EPA, which may promulgate federal standards if the state-level standards do not meet the requirements of the Clean Water Act. Dischargers into impaired waters, defined as water bodies that do not meet established water quality standards, may be subject to more stringent effluent limitations. Wastewater from the oil and gas production industry, which covers wastewater from the water produced from the geological formations and from chemicals used during exploration, well drilling and the production of oil and gas, must comply with discharge regulations promulgated in the EPA-developed Oil and Gas Extraction Guidelines and Standards. Additionally, indirect discharge from onshore facilities that deal in unconventional oil and gas extraction is prohibited. There are no such regulations for indirect discharge from conventional oil and gas extraction. Facilities located in the continental United States and west of the 98th meridian may discharge the produced water for use in agricultural or wildlife propagation. Class 2 waters are waters that are known to support fish or drinking water supplies. Class 2AB waters support both game fish populations or spawning and nursery areas and drinking water supplies, Class 2A waters are used for drinking water supplies, and Class 2B waters support game fish populations or spawning and nursery areas. Class 2C waters support only nongame fish populations. Class 2D waters support fish populations that would be significantly degraded if effluent flows were removed or reduced. Class 3 waters support aquatic life other than fish. Class 4 waters support agriculture, industry, recreation, and wildlife, but aquatic life uses are not attainable.
